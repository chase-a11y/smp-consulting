# I Built a Website and Nobody Visits It

You spent good money on a website. Maybe you built it yourself, maybe you hired someone. Either way, it's live, it looks decent, and it gets almost no traffic. You check the analytics once in a while, see the same flat line, and wonder if the whole thing was a waste.

It wasn't. But you're missing a critical piece that nobody told you about when you bought the site: a website is not a marketing strategy. It's a tool. And a tool that sits in a drawer doesn't do anything.

## Why doesn't "build it and they will come" work?

There are roughly 1.1 billion websites on the internet. Google has to decide which ones to show for any given search. If your site doesn't give Google a clear reason to recommend it, it won't. Simple as that.

Think about it from Google's perspective. Someone searches "best accountant in Springfield." Google needs to return results that are relevant, trustworthy, and useful. If your accounting firm's website has a homepage, an about page, and a contact form — and that's it — Google has almost nothing to work with. There's no content that matches the search, no signals that you're a credible source, and no reason to rank you above the dozens of other accountant websites in Springfield.

Having a website is table stakes. It's the bare minimum. What matters is whether that website is findable.

## What's the difference between having a site and having a findable site?

A website that just exists is like a billboard in your basement. It's technically there, but nobody can see it.

A findable site does three things:

1. **It speaks Google's language.** It has proper title tags, meta descriptions, heading structure, and content that matches what people actually search for.
2. **It answers real questions.** Every page serves a purpose and addresses something a potential customer is looking for.
3. **It's connected to the rest of the internet.** Other sites link to it. It's listed in directories. Google knows it's part of the broader web, not an island.

Most small business websites fail on all three. They were designed to look good (which matters for conversions) but nobody thought about how people would actually find them.

## What are title tags and meta descriptions, and do they really matter?

Yes, they matter. A lot.

Your title tag is the blue clickable text that shows up in Google search results. Your meta description is the gray text underneath it. Together, they're your first impression in search — and they tell Google what your page is about.

If your homepage title tag just says "Home" or your business name with no context, you're wasting prime real estate. A plumber in Austin whose title tag reads "Johnson Plumbing" is invisible to Google for searches like "emergency plumber Austin TX." But a title tag that reads "Emergency Plumber in Austin, TX — Johnson Plumbing" immediately tells both Google and the searcher what the page is about.

Every page on your site should have a unique, descriptive title tag and meta description. Not just the homepage — every page. This is one of the easiest SEO fixes and it's often completely overlooked.

## How do I set up Google Search Console?

If you don't have Google Search Console set up, stop reading and go do it. I mean that. It's free, it takes about 10 minutes, and it's the single most important tool for understanding how Google sees your website.

Here's the quick version:

1. Go to search.google.com/search-console
2. Add your website (you'll verify ownership by adding a small code snippet to your site or through your domain provider)
3. Submit your sitemap (usually yoursite.com/sitemap.xml)
4. Wait a few days for data to start showing up

Once it's connected, Search Console tells you:
- What search queries your site appears for
- How many times you showed up (impressions)
- How many times people clicked through
- Your average position for each query
- Any technical errors Google found while crawling your site

This data is gold. It shows you exactly where you stand and what to focus on.

## Why is content so important for traffic?

Every page on your website is a potential entry point from Google. If you have 5 pages, you have 5 chances to rank for something. If you have 50 pages of useful, well-written content, you have 50 chances.

This is why blogging works — not because the internet needs more blog posts, but because each post targets a specific question or topic that someone might search for. A landscaping company that publishes articles like "When to aerate your lawn in Ohio" or "How much does a paver patio cost" is creating dozens of entry points that bring people to their site.

But here's the key: the content has to be genuinely useful. Google has gotten very good at distinguishing between content written to help people and content written to game the algorithm. Write for the person searching, not for the robot. The robot will follow.

You don't need to publish every day. One solid, well-researched article per month is enough to start building momentum. Consistency matters more than volume.

## What else drives traffic besides SEO?

SEO (search engine optimization) is the long game. It builds over time and the results compound. But it's not the only way to get people to your website.

**Google Business Profile.** If you're a local business, your Google Business Profile can drive significant traffic. People who find you on Google Maps or in the local pack can click through to your website. Make sure your profile links to your site and that your site has the same information your profile does.

**Social media.** Not as a traffic firehose — organic social reach is pretty low these days — but as a way to stay visible to people who already know about you. Share your blog posts, link to your service pages, and give people a reason to visit your site.

**Email.** If you have a customer list (even a small one), sending a monthly email with useful content and a link back to your site is one of the most reliable traffic sources that exists. You own that audience. No algorithm can take it away.

**Directories and listings.** Yelp, industry-specific directories, your local chamber of commerce, BBB — these all drive referral traffic and help your SEO at the same time.

**Paid ads.** Google Ads and social media ads can drive traffic immediately, but they stop the moment you stop paying. I think of paid traffic as a supplement to organic traffic, not a replacement.

## What should I do this week?

If your site is getting no traffic, here's a one-week plan to start changing that:

- **Day 1:** Set up Google Search Console and submit your sitemap.
- **Day 2:** Audit every page's title tag and meta description. Rewrite any that are vague, missing, or just your business name.
- **Day 3:** Make a list of 10 questions your customers ask you regularly. These are future blog posts.
- **Day 4:** Write and publish one piece of content based on one of those questions. 500 to 800 words, answer-first, plain language.
- **Day 5:** Claim or update your Google Business Profile. Make sure it links to your website and has your correct business info.
- **Day 6-7:** Share that new piece of content on your social channels and send it to your email list if you have one.

That's it. Nothing fancy. No expensive tools. Just the fundamentals that most businesses skip.

## The takeaway

Your website isn't broken. It's just invisible. The fix is to give Google (and people) reasons to find it: clear metadata, useful content, proper technical setup, and connections to the rest of the web. None of this requires a big budget. It requires attention and consistency. Start with Search Console, fix your title tags, and write one good piece of content. Then do it again next month. Traffic doesn't happen by accident — but it does happen when you put in the work.
