You've done the hard work. You've asked happy customers for reviews, and they've come through. You're sitting at 4.7 stars on Google with dozens — maybe hundreds — of solid reviews. People clearly love what you do.

But those reviews aren't moving the needle. New customers aren't mentioning them. They don't seem to be a factor in your growth. The reviews just sit there on your Google listing, doing their thing passively, while you wonder what more they could be doing.

The answer: a lot more. Most small businesses treat reviews as a "set it and forget it" asset. They collect them on Google and never think about them again. That's leaving serious money on the table.

## Why aren't your Google reviews driving more business?

Because Google is only one of the places your reviews need to live.

Think about how someone finds and evaluates your business. Maybe they see your Google listing and glance at your star rating. That's good — it gets you in the door. But then they click through to your website. They land on your homepage. They look at your services page. They read your about page. And nowhere on your actual site do they see a single review.

Your Google reviews live on Google. Your website visitors are on your website. If you're not bringing those reviews onto your site, there's a trust gap between "this business looks decent on Google" and "I feel confident enough to fill out this form."

Social proof is most powerful at the moment of decision. When someone is on your landing page, about to fill out your contact form, a testimonial right next to that form can be the nudge that gets them to hit submit. A review sitting on Google three tabs over can't do that.

## Are your reviews actually visible where people make decisions?

Walk through your own website and count how many places you display customer reviews or testimonials. If the answer is zero, or if they're buried on a standalone "Testimonials" page that nobody visits, you're not using them effectively.

Reviews and testimonials should show up in strategic locations:

- **Homepage.** A section with 3-5 of your best reviews, ideally with names and context (what service they used, what problem you solved).
- **Service pages.** On each service page, include 1-2 reviews from customers who used that specific service. A review about your plumbing work belongs on your plumbing page, not mixed in with your HVAC reviews.
- **Landing pages.** Any page designed to capture leads should have social proof near the call to action. Reviews reduce friction at the exact moment someone is deciding whether to trust you with their contact info.
- **Contact page.** Right above or beside your contact form. This is where hesitation lives — "should I really reach out?" A few strong reviews answer that question.

## Do you have review schema markup on your site?

This is a technical one, but it matters. Schema markup is code you add to your site that tells search engines specific things about your content. Review schema tells Google "hey, this page has customer reviews, and here's the aggregate rating."

When done correctly, this can display star ratings directly in your Google search results — those gold stars you see under some search listings. Those stars increase click-through rates significantly because they catch the eye and build trust before someone even visits your site.

If you're not sure whether your site has review schema, search for your business on Google and look at your organic listing (not the map listing, but the regular website result below it). If there are no stars showing, you probably don't have schema markup in place.

Your web developer can add this, or if you're using a platform like WordPress, there are plugins that handle it. It's not complicated, but it's one of those things that's easy to overlook and genuinely moves the needle.

## Are you responding to your reviews?

Every review on your Google listing — positive and negative — should get a response. This isn't just good manners. It's visible to every future customer who reads your reviews.

When someone is scanning your reviews and sees that the business owner personally responds to each one, it signals that you're engaged, you care, and you're paying attention. When they see unanswered reviews — especially unanswered negative ones — it raises doubts.

For positive reviews, keep responses genuine and specific. Don't copy-paste the same generic "Thanks for your kind words" on every review. Reference something specific they mentioned. It shows you actually read it and you remember the interaction.

For negative reviews, respond calmly and professionally. Acknowledge the issue, explain what you've done or will do about it, and offer to continue the conversation offline. Future customers aren't just reading the negative review — they're reading your response to judge how you handle problems. A thoughtful response to a bad review can actually build more trust than a string of five-star ratings.

## Are you using reviews in your marketing?

Your reviews are marketing content. Customers wrote them for you. They're authentic, specific, and credible in a way that no ad copy you write yourself can be. And most businesses never use them beyond the Google listing.

Ways to put your reviews to work:

- **Social media posts.** Turn a great review into a graphic. Quote the review, add the customer's first name, overlay it on a simple branded background. This is some of the easiest and most effective social content you can create.
- **Email marketing.** Include a customer review in your newsletter or follow-up emails. A testimonial from a real customer carries more weight than anything you write about yourself.
- **Ad creative.** Use real customer quotes in your ads. "Don't take our word for it — here's what Sarah said after her first month." This is more believable than any claim you make about your own business.
- **Proposals and sales materials.** If you send proposals or quotes to prospects, include relevant testimonials. It validates your pricing and builds confidence.
- **In-person signage.** Print your best reviews and display them in your physical location. Waiting rooms, checkout counters, front windows — anywhere a prospective customer might be making a decision.

## What should you do this week?

You don't need to overhaul your entire marketing strategy. Start with the moves that have the most immediate impact:

1. **Add your top 5 reviews to your homepage.** Pull them from Google, format them with the customer's first name and what service they used, and put them in a visible section.
2. **Put a testimonial near every contact form.** Wherever you're asking someone to reach out, place a review nearby to reinforce their decision.
3. **Respond to your last 10 Google reviews.** Go through recent reviews you haven't responded to and write genuine, specific replies.
4. **Ask your developer about review schema.** Get those stars showing up in your search results.
5. **Turn one great review into a social media post** this week. Just one. See how it performs compared to your usual content.

Your reviews are one of your most valuable marketing assets. Right now they're probably working at about 20% of their potential. The fixes aren't hard — they just require you to actively use what you've already earned instead of letting it sit in one place.
