You Google your business and the name is slightly wrong. You check Yelp — different phone number. Apple Maps has your old address. Facebook has a variation of your name you stopped using two years ago.

This is one of the most common problems I see with small businesses, and most owners don't realize how much damage it's doing. Every wrong listing is a small crack in your credibility — with customers, with search engines, and increasingly, with AI systems that are trying to figure out who you are and whether to recommend you.

## What is NAP consistency and why does it matter?

NAP stands for Name, Address, Phone number. It's the most basic information about your business, and search engines use it as a trust signal. When Google sees the same name, same address, and same phone number repeated consistently across dozens of websites, it gains confidence that your business is real, active, and located where you say you are.

When that information is inconsistent — different name spellings, old addresses, wrong phone numbers — Google loses confidence. It's not sure which version is correct. And when Google isn't sure, it's less likely to show you in search results, especially in local pack results (those three businesses that show up with the map).

The math is simple: more consistency equals more trust equals better visibility.

## How do citations work?

A citation is any mention of your business name, address, and phone number on another website. Directory listings are the most obvious ones — Yelp, Yellow Pages, BBB — but citations also come from news articles, blog mentions, chamber of commerce sites, and industry-specific directories.

Search engines crawl these citations and cross-reference them. If 30 sites say your business is "Smith's Auto Repair" at 123 Main St and 5 sites say it's "Smiths Auto" at 125 Main St, Google has to decide which is right. Usually it goes with the majority, but those conflicting citations still drag down your overall trust score.

The problem compounds over time. You move locations, change phone numbers, rebrand slightly, or someone submits your info to a directory with a typo. Those old, wrong listings persist for years because nobody goes back to fix them.

## Which directories should I fix first?

Not all directories carry equal weight. Start with these — they're the ones that actually influence search rankings and feed data to other platforms:

1. **Google Business Profile** — This is the single most important listing you have. If nothing else, get this one perfect. Verify it, fill out every field, and keep it updated.
2. **Apple Maps** — Powers Siri and Apple device searches. More people use this than you'd think. Claim your listing through Apple Business Connect.
3. **Yelp** — Still a major citation source and ranks well in search results on its own.
4. **Facebook** — Your business page info gets crawled and cross-referenced. Make sure name, address, and phone match exactly.
5. **Bing Places** — Microsoft's search engine and Cortana pull from here. Also feeds into some AI assistants.
6. **Data aggregators** — Foursquare, Data Axle (formerly Infogroup), Localeze, and Neustar are the big four. These feed data to hundreds of smaller directories. Fix these and many downstream listings correct themselves over time.

After those, focus on industry-specific directories relevant to your business. A restaurant should care about TripAdvisor and OpenTable. A contractor should care about Angi and HomeAdvisor. Pick the ones your customers actually use.

## How do I find where my info is wrong?

You can do this manually by Googling your business name and checking the first few pages of results. But that's tedious and you'll miss a lot.

Better options:

- **Moz Local** — Free check that scans major directories and shows you where your info is inconsistent
- **BrightLocal** — More detailed audit with scoring; has a free trial
- **Semrush Listing Management** — Part of their paid toolkit but very thorough
- **Yext** — Does both scanning and fixing, though their subscription model is controversial (more on that another time)

Any of these will give you a clear picture of where your NAP data is wrong. From there, it's a matter of logging into each platform and correcting the information. Yes, it's tedious. But it's a one-time cleanup that pays dividends for years.

## How does inconsistent info hurt my SEO?

Local search ranking factors are well-documented at this point. Citation consistency is one of the top signals Google uses for local pack rankings. Studies from BrightLocal and Whitespark consistently rank NAP consistency in the top five factors for local search visibility.

When your info is inconsistent:

- Google may show the wrong address or phone number in search results
- Your local pack ranking drops because Google can't confidently verify your location
- Duplicate listings may appear, splitting your reviews and authority across multiple profiles
- Customers who find the wrong phone number or address have a bad experience before they even walk in the door

That last one is the real cost. Every wrong number is a lost customer. Every outdated address is someone driving to the wrong place and choosing your competitor instead.

## What does this mean for AI and voice search?

This is where it gets forward-looking. AI assistants — ChatGPT, Google's AI Overviews, Siri, Alexa — are increasingly answering questions about local businesses. When someone asks "what's a good plumber near me," these systems pull from the same data sources: Google Business Profile, Yelp, directory listings, your website.

If your information is inconsistent across those sources, the AI has less confidence in recommending you. It's called entity consistency — the AI needs to understand that all these different mentions refer to the same real-world business. Conflicting data makes that harder.

Businesses that have clean, consistent information across the web are easier for AI systems to understand, trust, and recommend. This isn't theoretical anymore. It's already happening.

## What should I do this week?

Run a free scan with Moz Local or BrightLocal. See where your info is wrong. Then fix your Google Business Profile first — that's the highest-impact change you can make in 15 minutes. After that, work through the major directories one by one.

It's not glamorous work. But cleaning up your business information across the web is one of the highest-ROI things you can do for your local visibility. Every listing you fix is one more signal telling search engines and AI systems: this business is real, it's here, and you can trust it.
