# My Newsletter Takes Me an Entire Saturday to Write

You sit down on Saturday morning to write your weekly newsletter. You stare at a blank screen. You write a paragraph. Delete it. Write another one. Check what your competitors are sending. Wonder if you need a header image. Tinker with the layout. Three hours later, you've got something mediocre that you're not even proud of, and your whole morning is gone.

I talk to small business owners about this all the time, and the story is always the same. The newsletter feels like a homework assignment. It takes way too long. And eventually, it just stops getting sent because who has that kind of time every week.

The problem isn't that newsletters are hard. The problem is that you've built an unnecessarily complicated process around something that should be simple.

## Why does my newsletter take so long?

Almost always, it comes down to one or more of these:

**You're trying to write a magazine article every time.** A newsletter doesn't need to be 1,500 words of polished prose. It's an email. People spend 30 to 60 seconds reading it. You're not writing for the New Yorker.

**You're starting from scratch every time.** No template, no structure, no repeatable format. Every issue is a blank canvas, which means every issue requires you to make dozens of decisions before you write a single word.

**You're overthinking the content.** What topic should I cover? Is this interesting enough? What if someone disagrees? What if it's too short? That internal debate eats more time than the actual writing.

**You're designing as you write.** Fiddling with images, colors, buttons, and formatting in the email builder is a separate task from writing. Doing both at the same time doubles the time and halves the quality of each.

## What newsletter format actually works for a small business?

The best newsletter format is one you can produce consistently without wanting to throw your laptop out the window. Here are a few proven structures that small businesses use successfully:

**The 3-2-1.** Three links you found useful this week, two quick tips related to your industry, one personal thought or observation. That's it. You can write this in 20 minutes once you get the hang of it.

**The single tip.** One useful idea, explained in 200 to 300 words. No filler, no preamble, just value. "Here's one thing I learned this week that might help you." Done.

**The roundup.** Curate content instead of creating it. Share three to five articles, tools, or resources relevant to your audience with a one-sentence take on each. You're adding value through curation, not original writing.

**The story + lesson.** Tell a quick story from your week — a client interaction, a mistake you made, something you noticed — and tie it to a takeaway. People remember stories. This format is personal, easy to write, and engaging.

**The FAQ answer.** Answer one question you get asked all the time. You probably answer the same 20 questions from customers over and over. Each one is a newsletter issue waiting to happen.

Pick one format and stick with it for at least a month. Consistency in structure makes the writing faster because you're not reinventing the wheel every time.

## How do I build a template I can reuse?

A template isn't about design — it's about structure. Create a reusable skeleton that you fill in each week. Here's what that looks like:

**Subject line:** [Fill in — use a formula like "One thing about [topic] that surprised me"]

**Opening line:** One to two sentences connecting with the reader. A question, a relatable observation, or a quick personal note.

**Body:** The meat of the content in your chosen format (tip, story, roundup, etc.)

**Closing:** One sentence wrapping it up. Optionally, a question to invite replies.

**P.S.:** A soft mention of something relevant — a product, a service, an event. Keep it casual.

Build this template once in your email platform. Save it. Every week, you duplicate it and swap in the content. No more starting from scratch. No more agonizing over layout.

## How can batching cut my newsletter time in half?

Batching means doing similar tasks in blocks instead of switching between them. For newsletters, that looks like:

**Content idea session (monthly).** Spend 30 minutes once a month brainstorming newsletter topics. Write down 4 to 5 ideas. Don't write them yet — just capture the ideas. Pull from customer questions, things you noticed in your business, industry trends, or conversations you've had.

**Writing block (weekly).** With your topic already chosen, sit down and write. No editing, no formatting. Just get the words down. Set a timer for 25 minutes and stop when it goes off. For a short-format newsletter, 25 minutes of focused writing is more than enough.

**Editing and formatting (same day or next day).** Come back with fresh eyes. Clean up the writing, drop it into your template, add any links, and schedule it. This should take 15 minutes max.

Total time per week: 40 to 45 minutes instead of half your Saturday.

## Can I use AI to help write my newsletter?

Yes, and you should — with a caveat.

AI is excellent for first drafts. If you give it your topic, your format, and some notes about what you want to say, it can produce a solid starting point in 30 seconds. That gets you past the blank page problem, which is where most of the time gets wasted.

Here's how to use it well:

1. **Start with a brain dump.** Jot down the key points you want to make in 3 to 5 bullet points. Don't worry about prose — just get your thoughts out.
2. **Feed it to your AI tool.** Use something like ChatGPT or Claude. Give it your bullet points and ask it to write a short newsletter draft in your style. Include a sample of a previous email you liked so it can match your voice.
3. **Edit for voice.** This is the step most people skip, and it's the most important one. Read the draft out loud. Change anything that doesn't sound like you. Add a personal detail or opinion that only you could provide. Cut anything that feels generic.

The AI does the heavy lifting. You add the personality. Total time: 20 to 30 minutes for the whole thing.

What you should not do is copy-paste an AI draft and hit send without reading it. Your subscribers will notice. It won't sound like you, it'll probably include some vague filler language, and over time it'll erode the trust you've built. AI writes the rough draft. You write the final one.

## What does a "good enough" newsletter look like?

Perfection is the enemy of consistency. A newsletter that goes out every week at 80% quality beats a "perfect" newsletter that goes out twice a quarter.

Here's what "good enough" looks like:

- It provides one piece of genuine value (a tip, a resource, a story, an answer)
- It sounds like you, not like a press release
- It's under 500 words (ideally 200 to 400)
- It took you less than an hour from start to scheduled
- It went out on time

That's it. Nobody is grading your newsletter. Your subscribers want to hear from you regularly, learn something occasionally, and feel like they're hearing from a real person. You don't need hero images. You don't need perfect grammar. You don't need eight sections with custom graphics.

You need to show up, say something useful, and hit send.

## The takeaway

If your newsletter is eating your weekend, you've built a process that's too heavy. Strip it down. Pick a simple format. Build a template you can reuse. Batch your work. Use AI for the first draft and add your own voice on top. Aim for "good enough" and ship it.

The newsletters that build real audience loyalty aren't the most polished. They're the most consistent. Figure out a process you can sustain every single week, even on your busiest weeks, and stick with it. That's the newsletter that actually grows your business.
