# My Business Doesn't Show Up on Google Maps

You Google your own business and it's not on the map. Or worse — you search for your type of business in your own city and three competitors show up in that little map box at the top of the results, but you're not one of them. You know you exist. Google apparently doesn't agree.

This is fixable. And for most local businesses, getting onto Google Maps and into the local "map pack" is the highest-impact thing you can do for your online visibility. It's often more valuable than your website ranking in the regular search results, because the map pack is what people see first — and it's where they click.

## Why am I not showing up on Google Maps?

There are really only a few reasons this happens:

1. **You haven't claimed your Google Business Profile.** Google may have created a bare-bones listing for your business based on public data, but if you haven't claimed it, you can't control what it says and Google has no reason to trust it enough to show it prominently.

2. **Your profile isn't verified.** Claiming and verifying are two different steps. Verification usually involves Google sending a postcard to your business address with a code, or verifying by phone or email. Until you complete this, your profile is in limbo.

3. **Your profile is incomplete.** Google favors profiles that are filled out thoroughly. A listing with just a name and address is going to lose to one that has categories, services, hours, photos, a description, and regular activity.

4. **Your business info is inconsistent across the web.** If your address is listed differently on your website, Yelp, Facebook, and your Google profile, Google gets confused about which one is correct. Confusion leads to lower confidence, which leads to lower visibility.

5. **You have very few (or no) reviews.** Reviews are one of the strongest local ranking signals. A business with zero reviews is a business that Google has no social proof for.

## How do I set up and verify my Google Business Profile?

If you haven't done this yet, here's the process:

1. Go to business.google.com and sign in with a Google account.
2. Search for your business. If a listing already exists, claim it. If not, create a new one.
3. Fill in your business name exactly as it appears in the real world. Don't stuff keywords into your business name — Google penalizes this.
4. Enter your address. If you serve customers at their location (mobile business, home services, etc.), you can set a service area instead.
5. Choose your primary business category. This is one of the most important fields. Pick the category that most precisely describes what you do. You can add secondary categories too.
6. Add your phone number and website URL.
7. Complete the verification process. The most common method is a postcard mailed to your business address with a PIN code. Some businesses qualify for phone, email, or instant verification.

Verification usually takes 5 to 14 days if you're going the postcard route. Don't skip this step. An unverified profile is essentially invisible.

## What is NAP consistency and why does it matter?

NAP stands for Name, Address, Phone number. It's the most basic information about your business, and it needs to be identical everywhere it appears online.

Not similar. Identical.

"123 Main Street, Suite 4" and "123 Main St #4" look the same to a human, but to Google's algorithms, they're different data points. When Google sees conflicting information across different sources, it loses confidence in your listing. Lower confidence means lower rankings.

Do an audit. Check your NAP on:
- Your website (header, footer, and contact page)
- Google Business Profile
- Yelp
- Facebook
- Apple Maps
- Any industry-specific directories
- BBB
- Your local chamber of commerce listing

Pick one format and make everything match exactly. This is tedious but it matters.

## How do categories and attributes affect my ranking?

Your primary category is one of the strongest ranking factors for Google Maps. If you're a pizza restaurant, your primary category should be "Pizza Restaurant," not "Restaurant" or "Italian Restaurant." Be as specific as possible.

You can also add secondary categories. A martial arts school might have "Martial Arts School" as primary, then add "Jiu Jitsu School" and "Kids Activity Provider" as secondary categories. These help you show up for a wider range of searches.

Attributes are the extras — things like "wheelchair accessible," "women-owned," "free Wi-Fi," "outdoor seating." They don't have as much ranking impact as categories, but they help your listing stand out and give potential customers useful information. Fill in everything that applies to your business.

## Do photos and posts really make a difference?

Yes. Businesses with photos on their Google Business Profile get 42% more requests for directions and 35% more clicks to their website than those without. That's Google's own data.

Upload real photos of your business:
- The exterior of your building (so people can find you)
- The interior
- Your team
- Your products or work in action
- Your logo and a cover photo

Avoid generic stock photos. People can tell, and it makes your business look less trustworthy.

Google Business posts are like mini social media updates that appear on your listing. You can share offers, events, updates, or articles. Posts expire after 7 days (events last until the event date), so the idea is to post regularly. Even one post per week signals to Google that your business is active and engaged. Most of your competitors aren't posting at all, so this is an easy way to get ahead.

## How much do reviews affect my Google Maps ranking?

A lot. Reviews are consistently ranked as one of the top three factors for local pack rankings, alongside relevance and proximity.

What Google looks at with reviews:

- **Volume** — More reviews generally means more visibility. A business with 60 reviews will typically outrank one with 6, all else being equal.
- **Recency** — Recent reviews matter more than old ones. A business that got 30 reviews three years ago and nothing since looks stagnant. Google wants to see ongoing activity.
- **Rating** — Higher is better, but perfection isn't required. A 4.6 with 100 reviews often outranks a 5.0 with 8 reviews.
- **Response** — Google can see whether you respond to reviews. Responding to every review (positive and negative) shows that you're engaged and care about your customers. It's also a ranking signal.
- **Keywords in reviews** — When customers naturally mention your services or location in their reviews, it reinforces your relevance for those searches. You can't control what people write, but you can ask satisfied customers to mention the specific service they used.

The easiest way to get more reviews: ask. Right after a positive interaction, send a direct link to your Google review page. Make it as frictionless as possible — one click, they're there. Most people are happy to leave a review. They just don't think to do it on their own.

## What are the local pack ranking factors in plain English?

Google's local pack (the map with three business listings at the top of local search results) is influenced by three main things:

1. **Relevance** — How well does your profile match what was searched? This is where categories, services, and your business description come in.
2. **Distance** — How far is your business from the person searching? You can't change your location, but you can make sure Google knows your correct address and service area.
3. **Prominence** — How well-known and well-regarded is your business? This is driven by reviews, backlinks, online mentions, and the strength of your website.

You can't control distance. But you can absolutely control relevance and prominence by optimizing your profile, building reviews, and keeping your online presence consistent and active.

## What should I do right now?

If you're not on Google Maps or you're buried below competitors, here's your action plan:

1. **Claim and verify your Google Business Profile** if you haven't already. This is non-negotiable.
2. **Complete every field.** Business description, categories, services, hours, attributes — all of it.
3. **Upload at least 10 real photos.** Exterior, interior, team, work samples.
4. **Audit your NAP** across every online listing and make them all match exactly.
5. **Start asking for reviews** this week. Set a goal of getting 5 new Google reviews in the next 30 days.
6. **Post to your profile once a week.** Share a tip, an update, a seasonal offer — anything to show activity.
7. **Respond to every existing review** you haven't responded to yet.

## The takeaway

Google Maps visibility isn't a mystery and it isn't luck. It's a direct result of how complete, consistent, and active your online presence is. Your Google Business Profile is the foundation. If it's unclaimed, incomplete, or neglected, you're handing those map pack spots to your competitors. The fix is straightforward — claim it, fill it out, get reviews, and stay active. Most of this can be done in an afternoon, and the results show up faster than almost any other marketing effort you can make.
