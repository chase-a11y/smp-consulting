You check your analytics and the numbers are brutal. People land on your site, stay for a second or two, and leave. No clicks, no scrolling, no contact form submissions. Just gone.

This is one of the most common problems I see with small business websites, and the good news is it's almost always fixable once you know what's driving people away.

## What does bounce rate actually mean?

Bounce rate is the percentage of visitors who land on your site and leave without doing anything — no clicking to another page, no filling out a form, nothing. They showed up and immediately decided this wasn't worth their time.

A bounce rate under 40% is solid. Between 40-60% is average. Above 60%, something is wrong. Above 80%, you have a serious problem that's actively costing you business.

The tricky part is that a high bounce rate doesn't tell you *why* people are leaving. It just tells you they are. So you have to look at the usual suspects.

## Is your site too slow?

This is the number one killer and the easiest to diagnose. If your site takes more than 3 seconds to load, you're losing roughly half your visitors before they ever see your content. That's not an exaggeration — Google's own research backs this up.

The 3-second rule is real. People's patience for a loading screen is basically nonexistent. They clicked a link expecting information, and if they're staring at a blank screen or a spinning wheel, they'll hit the back button and go to the next result.

Common speed killers:

- Oversized images that haven't been compressed
- Too many plugins or scripts loading at once
- Cheap shared hosting that can't handle traffic
- No caching set up
- Videos set to autoplay on the homepage

Run your site through Google PageSpeed Insights (it's free). It'll give you a score and tell you exactly what's slowing things down. If your mobile score is below 50, that's your starting point.

## Can visitors tell what you do in 3 seconds?

Imagine someone lands on your homepage for the first time. They've never heard of your business. Within 3 seconds, can they answer these questions:

1. What does this business do?
2. Who is it for?
3. What should I do next?

If the answer is no, you have a value proposition problem. And this is more common than you'd think.

I've seen sites where the homepage opens with a giant stock photo, a vague tagline like "Empowering Solutions for Tomorrow," and no indication of what the business actually sells. That visitor is gone. They don't have time to scroll down and figure it out.

Your above-the-fold content — the part people see before scrolling — needs to do the heavy lifting. A clear headline that says what you do, a short supporting line that says who it's for, and a button that tells them what to do next.

## Is your navigation confusing?

When someone lands on your site and can't figure out where to go, they leave. It's that simple.

Common navigation problems:

- Too many menu items (keep it to 5-7 max)
- Clever or creative labels instead of obvious ones (don't call your services page "What We Bring to the Table")
- No clear hierarchy — everything feels equally important, so nothing stands out
- Dropdown menus with 30 sub-items
- Important pages buried two or three clicks deep

Your navigation should feel obvious. Someone should be able to glance at your menu and immediately know where to find what they need. If you have to explain your menu structure to someone, it's too complicated.

## Are you hitting visitors with a wall of text?

People don't read websites the way they read books. They scan. They skim headlines, look at bullet points, glance at images, and decide in a few seconds whether to keep going.

If your homepage is dense paragraphs of text with no visual breaks, people's eyes glaze over and they leave. It doesn't matter how good the writing is — the formatting is pushing them away before they read a word.

Break up your content:

- Use short paragraphs (2-3 sentences max)
- Add subheadings that tell a story on their own
- Use bullet points and numbered lists for scannable info
- Include images or icons that support your message
- Leave white space — let the page breathe

## Does your site work on phones?

More than 60% of web traffic is mobile. If your site looks broken, loads slowly, or is hard to use on a phone, you're turning away the majority of your visitors.

Pull out your phone right now and load your website. Try to navigate it. Try to fill out your contact form. Try to tap your phone number to call. If any of that feels clunky, your mobile visitors are bouncing.

I'll cover mobile issues in more depth in a separate piece, but for now, just know that if you haven't checked your site on an actual phone recently, that might be your entire problem.

## What should you fix first?

If you're dealing with a high bounce rate, don't try to fix everything at once. Start with these in order:

1. **Check your speed.** Run PageSpeed Insights and fix the biggest issues first. This alone can cut your bounce rate significantly.
2. **Fix your above-the-fold content.** Make sure visitors know what you do within 3 seconds.
3. **Simplify your navigation.** Cut anything that isn't essential.
4. **Break up your text.** Add headings, bullet points, and white space.
5. **Test on mobile.** If it's broken on phones, that's likely where most of your bounces are coming from.

The pattern here is simple: people leave when something about your site makes them work too hard. They don't want to wait for it to load, figure out what you do, or decode your navigation. Make it easy and they'll stick around long enough to become a customer.
