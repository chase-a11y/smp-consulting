# Everything I Write Sounds Like a Robot Wrote It

You started using AI to help with your writing. Social posts, emails, product descriptions, maybe even blog content. At first, it felt like a miracle — content that used to take you hours was showing up in seconds. But then you read it back. And it all sounds the same. Generic. Stiff. Like a corporate press release written by someone who's never actually run a business.

Your customers can tell too. That vaguely inspirational, overly polished tone that AI defaults to has become instantly recognizable. People are developing an eye for it, and when your content reads like everyone else's, you lose the one thing that made your brand different: you.

The good news is that AI is still incredibly useful for writing. You just need to stop using it wrong.

## Why does AI default to that generic voice?

AI language models are trained on massive amounts of text from the internet. That includes marketing blogs, corporate communications, academic papers, and news articles. So when you ask it to "write a blog post about customer service," it pulls from that average. The result is technically correct, well-structured, and completely devoid of personality.

It's not the AI's fault. It's doing exactly what it was designed to do — produce the most statistically likely next word based on everything it's been trained on. The problem is that "most likely" equals "most average." And average is forgettable.

AI also has specific verbal tics. You'll notice patterns like:

- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "It's important to note that..."
- Excessive use of words like "leverage," "elevate," "robust," "streamline," and "harness"
- Every paragraph ending with a neat, tidy summary
- Lists that always have exactly five items
- An inspirational closing paragraph that says nothing specific

Once you spot these patterns, you can't unsee them. Neither can your readers.

## How should I actually use AI for writing?

Think of AI as your first-draft machine, not your finished-product machine. Its job is to get ideas out of your head and onto the screen. Your job is to make those ideas sound like they came from a human — specifically, you.

Here's the workflow that works:

**Step 1: Brain dump.** Before you touch AI, spend 3 to 5 minutes jotting down what you actually want to say. Bullet points. Fragments. Half-formed thoughts. The messy, unpolished version that lives in your head. This is your raw material.

**Step 2: Feed it to AI with context.** Don't just say "write me a blog post about X." Give it your bullet points, tell it who the audience is, and share an example of something you've written before that you liked. The more context you provide, the closer the output will be to your voice.

**Step 3: Treat the output as a rough draft.** Read through what AI produced. Keep the structure and any points that landed well. Then rewrite — don't just edit — the parts that don't sound like you. Add specific examples from your own experience. Remove the generic filler. Cut the corporate-speak.

**Step 4: Read it out loud.** This is the most important step and the one most people skip. Read the final version out loud, as if you were saying it to a customer standing in front of you. If any sentence makes you cringe, rewrite it. If you'd never say it in conversation, take it out.

## What does before-and-after look like?

Here's an AI-generated paragraph for a small bakery:

*"At our bakery, we are committed to delivering an exceptional culinary experience through our artisanal baked goods. Each item is meticulously crafted using premium ingredients to ensure the highest quality and customer satisfaction."*

Nobody talks like that. Here's the same idea in a human voice:

*"We bake everything from scratch every morning. Real butter, real vanilla, no shortcuts. If it doesn't taste like something your grandmother would be proud of, it doesn't go in the case."*

Same message. Completely different feeling. The second version has personality, specificity, and a point of view. The first one could belong to any bakery on earth.

Another example. AI-generated email subject line:

*"Discover Our Latest Collection of Premium Products"*

Human version:

*"Three new items that customers won't stop asking about"*

The AI version is vague and corporate. The human version is specific and creates curiosity. The difference isn't talent — it's knowing what to keep and what to throw away.

## What makes writing sound like a real person?

A few things that AI consistently gets wrong and humans consistently get right:

**Specificity.** AI speaks in generalities. Humans use specific details. "We serve great coffee" vs. "We use a single-origin Ethiopian roast from a farm in Yirgacheffe." Details build trust and credibility.

**Opinions.** AI hedges everything. "There are many approaches to consider." Humans take a stance. "Here's what I'd actually do." Your audience wants your perspective, not a balanced review of all possible options.

**Imperfection.** AI writes in perfectly polished paragraphs with smooth transitions. Real writing has short sentences. Fragments sometimes. A tone that varies. The slight messiness is what makes it feel authentic.

**Stories.** AI can generate fictional stories, but it can't tell your stories. The client who called you at 11 PM. The time you completely botched a project and learned from it. The weird interaction that taught you something about your industry. Those are yours. Use them.

**Contractions and casual language.** AI tends toward formal: "do not," "it is," "we are." Most people write the way they talk: "don't," "it's," "we're." If your writing is more formal than your speaking, it doesn't sound like you.

## How do I give AI better prompts to match my voice?

The prompt you use determines the output you get. Here are a few techniques:

**Include a voice sample.** Paste in a paragraph or email you've written before that you think captures your voice. Tell the AI: "Match this tone and style."

**Describe your voice in concrete terms.** Instead of "write in a friendly tone," try "write like a business owner talking to another business owner over coffee. Casual but knowledgeable. No jargon. Short sentences. Occasional dry humor."

**Tell it what NOT to do.** "Don't use words like leverage, elevate, harness, or streamline. Don't start with 'In today's world.' Don't end with an inspirational call to action. No corporate-speak."

**Give it constraints.** "Keep it under 200 words. Use sentences under 15 words. No bullet lists — just flowing paragraphs." Constraints force the AI to deviate from its defaults.

**Edit the output and feed it back.** After you rewrite the first draft in your voice, show the AI your edited version and say "this is closer to what I want — use this version as the style guide going forward." Over time, it gets better at matching your voice.

## The takeaway

AI is a tool, not a ghostwriter. Use it to skip the blank page, generate structure, and produce a rough first draft. Then do the human work: inject your stories, add your opinions, cut the generic filler, and read it out loud until it sounds like something you'd actually say.

The businesses that use AI well aren't the ones that publish the most content. They're the ones that use AI to work faster while keeping their voice intact. Speed without personality is just noise. Personality without speed burns you out. The combination of both is what you're after.
