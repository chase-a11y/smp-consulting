You know your prices are too low. You've known for a while. Your costs went up, your skills improved, your results got better. But every time you think about raising your rates, a knot forms in your stomach. You picture the email from a long-time customer asking why. You imagine people leaving. So you leave the prices where they are and quietly resent the work.

This is one of the most common problems I see with small business owners, and it's almost never a math problem. It's an emotional one. Let's untangle it.

## Why does raising prices feel so personal?

Because for most small business owners, it is personal. You're not a faceless corporation adjusting a SKU in a database. You know your customers by name. You've seen their kids grow up. You've done them favors, cut them deals, stayed late.

So when you think about charging more, it feels like you're betraying the relationship. Like you're being greedy. Like you're taking advantage of people who trust you.

Here's what's actually happening: you're confusing your personal relationship with your customer with the business transaction between you. Those are two different things. You can genuinely care about someone and also charge them a fair price for your work. In fact, you have to, because if you don't charge enough, you won't be in business long enough to keep helping them.

## What's the real cost of underpricing?

Underpricing doesn't just mean you make less money. It creates a cascade of problems:

- **You cut corners to stay profitable.** When margins are thin, you can't afford to deliver your best work. Quality drops. Ironically, the thing you were trying to protect -- the customer relationship -- suffers.
- **You attract price-sensitive customers.** Low prices draw people who are shopping on price alone. These are usually your most demanding, least loyal customers. They'll leave the moment someone undercuts you by five dollars.
- **You resent the work.** When you feel underpaid, motivation drops. You start dreading projects. Burnout follows. I've seen talented people quit businesses they loved because they couldn't bring themselves to charge what they were worth.
- **You can't invest in growth.** No margin means no money for better tools, better marketing, better help. You stay stuck.

Underpricing isn't generosity. It's a slow leak in the hull of your business.

## Should I price based on cost or value?

Most small businesses start with cost-based pricing: figure out what it costs to deliver, add a margin, that's the price. This works when you're starting out and need a floor. But it caps your growth, because it ties your prices to your expenses rather than to the results you deliver.

Value-based pricing flips it around. Instead of asking "what does this cost me to deliver," you ask "what is this worth to the customer?" A website that generates $50,000 in new business for a client is worth more than the 40 hours it took you to build it. A training program that reduces employee turnover by 30% is worth more than the cost of the materials.

You don't have to go full value-based overnight. But start thinking about outcomes, not hours. When you frame your pricing around what the customer gets rather than what it costs you, the conversation changes entirely.

## How do I actually communicate a price increase?

This is where most people get stuck. The decision to raise prices is hard enough. Telling people about it feels impossible. Here's a straightforward approach:

1. **Give notice.** Don't surprise anyone. Two to four weeks is usually enough. For larger increases or long-term clients, a month or more is respectful.
2. **Be direct and brief.** Don't write a three-paragraph apology. Something like: "Starting [date], our rates for [service] will be [new price]. This reflects [one brief reason: increased costs, expanded service, market adjustment]." That's it.
3. **Don't over-explain.** The more you justify, the more it sounds like you're asking permission. You're not. You're informing.
4. **Lead with what hasn't changed.** Remind them of the value they're getting. "You'll still get [specific deliverable/result], and we're also adding [any new benefit if applicable]."
5. **Be ready for questions, but don't preemptively discount.** Some people will ask if there's a way to keep the old rate. Have your answer ready before they ask. Which brings us to the next question.

## Should I grandfather existing customers or do a clean break?

Both approaches work. The choice depends on your business model and how much the increase is.

**Grandfathering** (keeping existing customers at the old rate for a period) works well when the increase is significant and you have long-term recurring relationships. It shows loyalty and softens the transition. The downside: you now have two (or more) pricing tiers to manage, and the longer you grandfather, the bigger the gap between old and new customers.

**Clean breaks** (everyone moves to the new price on the same date) are simpler to manage and prevent the pricing tier problem. They work best when the increase is modest or when you're repositioning your service.

A middle path: grandfather existing customers for 90 days, then move everyone to the new rate. This gives loyal customers a grace period without creating a permanent two-tier system.

## What about the customers I'll lose?

You will lose some customers. That part is real, and pretending otherwise would be dishonest. But here's the pattern I see over and over: the customers who leave over a modest price increase are almost always the ones who were the hardest to serve. The ones who haggled, complained, asked for extras, and took the most energy.

Your best customers -- the ones who value your work, refer others, and are a pleasure to deal with -- almost never leave over a reasonable price increase. They understand that good work costs money. They've probably been wondering why you were so cheap.

I've watched business owners raise prices by 15-20% and lose maybe 5% of their customer base. The remaining 95% pays more, the lost 5% was the most difficult segment, and net revenue goes up significantly. The business gets healthier and the owner gets happier. Every time.

## What if I'm still not sure I'm worth the higher price?

Then this isn't a pricing problem. It's a confidence problem. And the fix isn't more market research or competitive analysis. The fix is looking honestly at the results you deliver.

Write down the last five projects you completed or customers you served. What did they get? What changed for them because of your work? If you can point to real outcomes -- more revenue, time saved, problems solved, goals reached -- then your prices should reflect that.

Your prices are a statement about how you value your own work. If you undercharge, you're telling the market that what you do isn't that important. And eventually, the market will believe you.

## The takeaway

Raising prices is uncomfortable. It probably always will be a little. But staying underpriced is more than uncomfortable -- it's unsustainable. You deserve to be paid fairly for good work, and your customers deserve a business that can afford to keep delivering it.

Pick a date. Set the new price. Send the notice. The knot in your stomach will be gone in a week. The extra margin will be there every month after.
