# Why Does My Competitor Show Up on Google and I Don't?

You search for your own service in your own town and your competitor is sitting right there at the top. You're nowhere. You have a website. You have a business. You do great work. So what gives?

This is one of the most frustrating things a small business owner deals with, and it's more common than you think. The good news is that it's not random. Google has reasons for ranking one business over another, and once you understand what those reasons are, you can start closing the gap.

## How does Google decide who ranks first?

Google uses over 200 factors to decide where a page shows up in search results, but you don't need to worry about all of them. For local small businesses, it mostly comes down to a handful of things:

- **Relevance** — Does your website clearly match what the person searched for?
- **Authority** — Does Google trust your site? Have other reputable sites linked to yours?
- **Experience** — When people land on your site, do they stick around or bounce?
- **Proximity** — How close is your business to the person searching?

Your competitor is probably beating you on one or more of these. Let's break down the most likely reasons.

## What is domain authority and why does it matter?

Domain authority is a concept (originally coined by Moz, but the principle applies across all SEO tools) that measures how much trust and credibility your website has built up over time. It's scored on a scale from 0 to 100.

A business that's had a website for 10 years, with dozens of other websites linking to it, is going to have a higher authority score than a site that went live six months ago. That's not a death sentence for newer sites — it just means you need to be more intentional about building credibility.

Think of it like a reputation. A new restaurant in town might have the best food, but if nobody's heard of it yet and the place down the street has been reviewed in every local publication for years, guess which one people trust more. Google works the same way.

## What is my competitor actually doing that I'm not?

I've audited hundreds of small business websites. Here's what the ones that rank well tend to have in common:

**They have real content on their pages.** Not just a homepage with a logo and a phone number. They have pages that explain their services, answer common questions, and include the words people actually search for. If your competitor has a dedicated page for "emergency plumbing repair in [your city]" and you just have a bullet point that says "emergency services," they win that search.

**They've claimed and optimized their Google Business Profile.** This is the single biggest lever for local search. If your competitor has a complete profile with photos, reviews, business hours, service descriptions, and regular posts — and yours is either unclaimed or half-filled-out — they're going to dominate the local map pack.

**They have reviews.** Google treats reviews as a trust signal. A business with 85 reviews averaging 4.7 stars is going to outrank a business with 3 reviews, even if the 3-review business does better work. Volume and recency both matter.

**They use structured data (schema markup).** This is a bit technical, but it matters. Schema markup is code on your website that tells Google exactly what your business is, where it's located, what services you offer, and more. It's like handing Google a cheat sheet instead of making it guess. Most small business websites don't have this. The ones that do get an edge.

**They have backlinks.** Other websites link to theirs. Maybe they were featured in a local news article, listed in a chamber of commerce directory, or mentioned on a partner's website. Each of those links is a vote of confidence in Google's eyes.

## How do I figure out where I stand right now?

Before you start fixing things, you need to know your starting point. Here's a quick self-audit:

1. **Google yourself.** Search for your main service + your city. Note where you show up (if at all) and who's above you.
2. **Check your Google Business Profile.** Go to business.google.com and make sure your listing is claimed, verified, and fully filled out.
3. **Look at your competitor's site.** Not to copy it, but to notice what they have that you don't. Do they have more pages? More content? A blog?
4. **Count your reviews vs. theirs.** On Google specifically. Other platforms matter too, but Google reviews directly affect Google rankings.
5. **Set up Google Search Console.** This free tool from Google tells you exactly what searches your site appears for, how often, and where you rank. If you don't have this set up, you're flying blind.

## What should I do first to start catching up?

You don't need to do everything at once. Here's where I'd start, in order of impact:

1. **Claim and fully complete your Google Business Profile.** Add every service, upload real photos (not stock), write a solid business description, set your hours, and pick the right categories. This alone can move the needle significantly.

2. **Ask for reviews.** Start a simple habit: after every good interaction with a customer, send them a direct link to leave a Google review. Don't be weird about it. Just make it easy for them.

3. **Add more content to your website.** Create a dedicated page for each core service you offer. Write like you're explaining it to a customer, not like you're writing a brochure. Include your city and service area naturally in the text.

4. **Get listed in local directories.** Your city's chamber of commerce, industry-specific directories, Yelp, BBB — these are easy backlinks and they help Google verify that your business is real and consistent across the web.

5. **Make sure your name, address, and phone number (NAP) are identical everywhere.** If your website says "123 Main St" but your Google listing says "123 Main Street," that inconsistency can hurt you. Pick one format and use it everywhere.

## How long does it take to see results?

I won't sugarcoat this: SEO is not instant. If your competitor has a five-year head start, you're not going to overtake them in a week. But most small businesses start seeing measurable improvement within 3 to 6 months of consistent effort. Some changes, like optimizing your Google Business Profile, can show results in days.

The businesses that win at this aren't the ones with the biggest budgets. They're the ones that show up consistently — adding a review here, publishing a page there, keeping their information accurate. It compounds over time.

## The takeaway

Your competitor isn't ranking above you because Google likes them better. They're ranking because they've given Google more reasons to trust them. The fix isn't complicated, but it does require effort. Start with your Google Business Profile, build up your reviews, create content that matches what your customers are searching for, and stay consistent. The gap will close faster than you think.
