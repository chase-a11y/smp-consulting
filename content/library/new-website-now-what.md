The website is live. You refreshed the page about fifteen times just to make sure. It looks great. You sent the link to a few friends. They said it looks great too. Now you're sitting there wondering why the phone isn't ringing.

Here's the thing nobody tells you when you launch a website: going live is the starting line, not the finish line. A brand new website with no traffic, no search presence, and no content strategy is a digital business card sitting in a drawer. It exists, but nobody's looking at it.

The good news is that the first 30-90 days after launch are when you build the foundation that makes the site actually work. And it's not complicated. It's just a checklist.

## What should I do in the first week?

Before you think about marketing or content, make sure the technical basics are in place. These are one-time setup tasks that most people skip or forget, and every week you wait is a week your site isn't being properly indexed or tracked.

**Google Search Console.** Go to search.google.com/search-console and add your site. This is how Google knows your website exists. Submit your sitemap (usually at yourdomain.com/sitemap.xml). This tells Google every page on your site and how they're organized. Without this, Google will eventually find you, but it could take weeks or months.

**Google Analytics.** If your web developer didn't install it, do it now. Google Analytics (GA4) is free and tells you who's visiting your site, where they came from, and what they do when they get there. More importantly, set up at least one conversion event -- a form submission, a phone call click, or a purchase. Traffic without conversion tracking is just a number going up.

**Google Business Profile.** If you have a physical location or serve a local area, claim and complete your Google Business Profile at business.google.com. Add your website URL, your hours, your phone number, photos, and a description. This is how you show up in Google Maps and local search results. Link it to your website. This is one of the single most impactful things a local business can do online, and it's free.

**Social media links.** Update every social profile you have -- Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn, whatever you use -- with your new website URL. Not just in the bio. Create a post announcing the new site. Pin it if the platform allows it. These links serve two purposes: they drive direct traffic from people who already follow you, and they create backlinks that help with SEO over time.

## What matters in the first 30 days?

The first month is about getting indexed and establishing a baseline. Here's what to focus on:

**Get your first 10 pages indexed.** Google doesn't index your entire site overnight. Check Search Console's "Pages" report to see which pages Google has discovered and indexed. If key pages aren't showing up after a week or two, request indexing manually through Search Console. Your homepage, service pages, about page, and contact page should all be indexed within the first month.

**Tell your existing customers.** This one gets overlooked constantly. You already have people who know and trust you. Send them an email. Mention it in conversation. Put the URL on your invoices, your email signature, your business cards, your receipts. Your existing network is the fastest source of initial traffic, and their visits signal to Google that your site is legitimate.

**Verify your site works on mobile.** Pull up your site on your phone. Actually use it. Fill out the form. Click the phone number. Try to find your hours. More than half your visitors will be on mobile, and if the experience is clunky, they'll leave. Google also uses mobile experience as a ranking factor.

**Check your page speed.** Go to pagespeed.web.dev and enter your URL. If your score is below 50 on mobile, there are probably quick fixes -- oversized images, unused scripts, missing compression. Your developer can address these, or you can use the specific recommendations Google provides. Speed affects both user experience and search rankings.

## What about content -- do I need a blog?

You don't need to become a blogger. But you do need more than five static pages if you want to show up in search results for anything beyond your business name.

Think of content this way: every page on your site is an opportunity to rank for a specific question or topic that your potential customers are searching for. Your homepage ranks for your business name. Your service pages rank for your core offerings. But what about the dozens of questions people ask before they're ready to buy?

A plumber who publishes an article answering "why is my water heater making noise" is going to show up when a homeowner searches that question at 10 PM. That homeowner now knows the plumber exists. If the article is helpful, they might call that plumber instead of a competitor.

You don't need to publish every day or every week. Start with one piece of content per month that answers a real question your customers ask you. After a year, you'll have 12 pages of useful content that bring in search traffic you'd never get from your homepage alone.

## What should I expect in the first 90 days?

Set realistic expectations so you don't get discouraged. Here's what a normal first 90 days looks like:

**Month 1:** Minimal organic traffic. Most of your visitors will come from direct links (you sending people the URL) and social media. Google is still discovering and evaluating your site. This is normal.

**Month 2:** You'll start to see some search impressions in Google Search Console -- meaning Google is showing your pages in results, even if nobody is clicking yet. Your pages are being tested. If you've published any content, a few long-tail queries might start bringing in traffic.

**Month 3:** If you've been consistent with the basics -- site is fast, content is being added, Google Business Profile is active -- you'll start to see gradual organic traffic growth. Not a flood. A trickle. That trickle compounds over time.

The 90-day SEO ramp is real. Search engines need time to trust a new domain. There's no shortcut around this. Paid ads can fill the gap during these first three months if you need immediate traffic, but organic growth is a long game that pays off more and more as your site ages and gains authority.

## What are the biggest mistakes people make after launching?

Three things I see over and over:

**They stop updating the site.** The site launches, looks great, and then nothing changes for a year. Google notices. A site that never updates signals that it might not be active or relevant. Even small updates -- a new photo, an updated service description, a blog post -- keep the site fresh in Google's eyes.

**They don't track anything.** The site is live but nobody's checking whether it's working. No analytics, no conversion tracking, no Search Console. You can't improve what you don't measure. Spend 15 minutes per month reviewing your numbers. It's enough.

**They expect instant results.** Someone launches a site on Monday and is disappointed by Friday that they're not on the first page of Google. SEO takes months, not days. If you need immediate visibility, invest in Google Ads or local service ads while your organic presence builds. The website is the foundation. Marketing is what drives people to it.

## The takeaway

Your new website is the starting line. The first 30 days are about getting the technical foundation right -- Search Console, Analytics, Business Profile, speed, mobile. The first 90 days are about establishing a baseline, publishing your first pieces of content, and letting Google start to trust your domain.

There's no magic trick. It's a checklist and a calendar. Set up the tools this week. Tell your existing customers this month. Publish one piece of content per month. Review your numbers quarterly. In six months, you'll have a site that actually works for your business, not just one that looks nice.

The website is built. Now the real work starts.
