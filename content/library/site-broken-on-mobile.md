Pull out your phone and load your website right now. Actually do it. Tap through the menu, read a page, try to fill out your contact form, try to tap your phone number.

If anything felt awkward — text too small, buttons hard to tap, content cut off or scrolling sideways — you just experienced what more than half your visitors deal with every time they find you online.

## How much of your traffic is actually mobile?

More than you think. Across all industries, mobile accounts for roughly 60% of all web traffic. For local businesses — restaurants, salons, gyms, service providers — it's often 70% or higher.

Think about how people find local businesses. They're on their phone, they Google something like "electrician near me" or "best pizza downtown," they tap a result, and they're on your site. That entire journey happens on a 6-inch screen.

If your site was designed primarily for desktop and mobile was an afterthought, you're delivering a bad experience to the majority of your visitors. Not some of them. Most of them.

## What is responsive design and does your site have it?

Responsive design means your website automatically adjusts its layout based on the screen size. On a desktop monitor, you might see a three-column layout with a sidebar. On a phone, that same content reorganizes into a single column that's easy to scroll through.

A responsive site doesn't just shrink everything down. It reorganizes, resizes, and reprioritizes content so it works naturally on any device.

Here's how to check if your site is responsive:

- Open your site on your laptop browser
- Grab the edge of the browser window and slowly drag it narrower
- Watch what happens to the layout

If the content reorganizes smoothly — columns stack, text reflows, images resize — you have responsive design. If everything just gets smaller and eventually requires horizontal scrolling, you don't.

You can also run your URL through Google's Mobile-Friendly Test (search for it — it's a free tool). It'll tell you straight up whether Google considers your site mobile-friendly and flag specific issues.

## What are the most common mobile UX killers?

I see the same problems over and over on small business sites that haven't been properly optimized for mobile.

**Tiny tap targets.** Buttons and links that are too small or too close together. On a phone, people are tapping with their thumb, not clicking with a precise mouse cursor. If your buttons are small or packed tightly, visitors will tap the wrong thing, get frustrated, and leave. Buttons should be at least 44x44 pixels with enough space between them.

**Horizontal scrolling.** If visitors have to scroll sideways to see your content, something is broken. This usually happens when images are wider than the screen, tables don't collapse properly, or elements have fixed pixel widths instead of flexible ones. A mobile page should only scroll vertically.

**Popups that take over the screen.** On desktop, a popup might be mildly annoying. On mobile, it's a disaster. It fills the entire screen, the close button is microscopic, and half the time people accidentally tap the ad instead of dismissing it. If you're running popups, either disable them on mobile or make absolutely sure they're easy to close with a thumb tap.

**Text too small to read.** If visitors have to pinch and zoom to read your content, your font size is too small for mobile. Body text should be at least 16 pixels. Anything smaller and people are squinting or zooming, which is a terrible experience.

**Forms that are painful to fill out.** Long forms with tiny input fields are brutal on mobile. If your contact form has 8 fields and the text inputs are so small people can barely tap into them, you're losing leads. On mobile, keep forms short (name, email or phone, message) and make the input fields large enough to actually use.

**Unplayable or auto-playing media.** Videos that autoplay with sound, heavy animations that stutter on older phones, or media players that don't work on mobile browsers. If you have video on your site, make sure it's embedded properly (YouTube or Vimeo handles this well) and never autoplays with sound.

## Why does Google care about your mobile experience?

Google uses mobile-first indexing. That means when Google evaluates your site for search rankings, it looks at the mobile version first — not the desktop version.

If your desktop site is great but your mobile site is broken, Google is ranking you based on the broken version. This has been the case since 2019, and a lot of business owners still don't realize it.

So a bad mobile experience doesn't just lose you visitors who are on their phones. It can hurt your search rankings across the board, meaning fewer people find you on any device.

## How do you actually test your site on mobile?

Don't rely on just dragging your browser window narrower. That gives you a rough idea, but it doesn't replicate the real mobile experience.

Here's how to test properly:

1. **Use your actual phone.** Load your site on your personal phone. Navigate every page. Fill out your forms. Tap every button. This is the most honest test because it's the real experience.

2. **Use Chrome DevTools.** In Chrome on your laptop, right-click anywhere on your page, click "Inspect," then click the device toggle icon (looks like a phone and tablet). This lets you simulate different phone and tablet sizes. Test on iPhone SE (small screen), iPhone 14 (standard), and iPad.

3. **Test on both iPhone and Android.** If you only have one type of phone, ask a friend or family member with the other type to check your site and give you honest feedback.

4. **Try it on a slow connection.** Mobile users are often on spotty cell connections, not fast wifi. In Chrome DevTools, you can throttle your connection speed to simulate 3G. If your site is painfully slow on a throttled connection, that's what a chunk of your visitors experience.

5. **Watch someone else use it.** Hand your phone to someone who's never seen your site and ask them to find your phone number, or figure out what you do, or fill out your contact form. Watch where they struggle. Don't help them. Just watch.

## What should you do about it?

If your mobile experience is rough, here's the priority order:

1. **Make sure your site is responsive.** If it's not, this is a rebuild conversation, not a tweak. A non-responsive site in 2026 is essentially broken for the majority of your visitors.
2. **Fix your tap targets.** Make buttons and links big enough to tap easily with a thumb.
3. **Eliminate horizontal scrolling.** Find and fix whatever is causing content to extend beyond the screen width.
4. **Increase font size.** 16px minimum for body text on mobile.
5. **Simplify forms.** Cut fields down to the essentials and make input areas large.
6. **Kill intrusive popups** on mobile, or redesign them so they're easy to dismiss.
7. **Test your speed on mobile.** Run PageSpeed Insights and focus on the mobile score.

Your mobile site isn't a secondary version of your "real" website. For most of your visitors, it is your website. Treat it that way.
