# Customers Keep Asking Me Questions That Are Already on My Website

You spent time writing up your hours, your pricing, your service areas, your process — all of it is right there on the website. And yet the phone rings, the emails come in, and the DMs pile up with the same questions over and over. "What time do you close?" "Do you offer financing?" "Do you serve my area?"

It's tempting to be annoyed. But the reality is, this isn't your customers' fault. If people can't find the answers on your site, the answers might as well not exist. The information is there. The discoverability is not.

## Why can't people find what's already on my site?

There are a few common reasons, and usually it's a combination:

**The information is buried.** It's in the third paragraph of a page nobody scrolls to. Or it's on a page that's two or three clicks deep in your navigation. People don't dig. If the answer isn't obvious within a few seconds, they pick up the phone or leave.

**Your navigation doesn't match how people think.** You organized your site based on how your business is structured internally. But your customers don't know (or care about) your internal structure. They're thinking in terms of their own questions: "How much does this cost?" "Are you open on Saturday?" "What's included?" If your navigation labels don't match those mental models, people get lost.

**The page is a wall of text.** You wrote everything out in long paragraphs with no headings, no formatting, and no visual hierarchy. People scan web pages — they don't read them top to bottom like a book. If you're not formatting for scanners, most people will miss the answer even when they're staring right at it.

**Google isn't surfacing the right pages.** Even if the content is on your site, Google might not be showing it in search results for the relevant queries. If someone Googles "do you offer Saturday appointments" and your FAQ page doesn't rank for that query, they'll never find it through search.

## How should I structure my site navigation?

Your navigation should be built around what your customers are looking for, not what makes sense to you internally.

For most small businesses, the essential navigation items are:

- **Services** (or what you do) — with sub-pages for each major service
- **About** — who you are, your story, your team
- **Locations / Service Areas** — where you work
- **Pricing** (if applicable) — don't hide this if your pricing is straightforward
- **FAQ** — a dedicated page for common questions
- **Contact** — phone, email, form, hours, address

Beyond the main navigation, make sure important information is accessible from multiple entry points. Your hours should be in the header or footer of every page, not just buried on the contact page. Your phone number should be clickable on mobile. If you serve multiple locations, each should have its own page.

The test is simple: can a first-time visitor find the answer to their most common question within 10 seconds of landing on your site? If not, your navigation needs work.

## What makes a good FAQ page?

A FAQ page is one of the most underutilized pages on small business websites. Done right, it reduces repetitive inquiries, improves SEO, feeds AI answer engines, and makes your customers' lives easier. Done wrong (or not at all), and you're leaving all of that on the table.

Here's what a good FAQ page looks like:

**Use real questions.** Not questions you wish people would ask — questions they actually ask. Go through your emails, your DMs, your call logs. What do people ask you most? Those are your FAQs.

**Write the answer first.** Start each answer with the direct response, then elaborate if needed. Don't make people read three sentences of context before getting to the point.

Good:
> **Do you offer financing?**
> Yes. We offer 12-month financing through GreenSky with no interest for qualifying applicants. You can apply online or in-store, and approval usually takes less than 5 minutes.

Not good:
> **Do you offer financing?**
> We understand that budgeting for home improvements can be challenging, and we want to make sure our services are accessible to everyone. That's why we've partnered with several financing providers to offer flexible payment options...

**Organize by topic.** If you have more than 10 FAQs, group them. "Pricing & Payment," "Scheduling," "Services," "Getting Started." This makes it scannable.

**Add FAQPage schema markup.** This tells Google and AI tools that your page contains structured question-and-answer pairs. It can get your FAQs featured directly in search results and pulled into AI-generated answers. This is one of the highest-ROI schema types you can add.

## How does internal linking help people find answers?

Internal linking means linking from one page on your site to another. It sounds basic, but most small business websites barely do it, and it's causing real problems.

When you mention a service on your homepage, does it link to the dedicated service page? When your about page mentions your team's certifications, does it link to a page where people can learn more? When a blog post answers a question, does it link to the relevant FAQ?

Internal links do two things:

1. **They guide visitors.** They create pathways for people to find related information without having to go back to the main navigation and hunt for it. Every internal link is a breadcrumb that leads to another useful page.

2. **They help Google understand your site's structure.** Google discovers and indexes pages by following links. If a page on your site has no internal links pointing to it, Google may not even know it exists. That means it can't show that page in search results — even if it has the exact answer someone is looking for.

A good rule of thumb: every page on your site should be reachable within 2 to 3 clicks from your homepage, and every page should link to at least 2 or 3 other relevant pages.

## What is answer-first formatting and why does it matter?

Answer-first formatting is exactly what it sounds like: you lead with the answer, then provide supporting detail. It's the inverted pyramid structure that journalists have used for a century, applied to web content.

This matters for three reasons:

**People scan.** Eye-tracking studies consistently show that web visitors read in an F-pattern — they scan the top of the page and the left edge, looking for key information. If your answer is buried in the middle of a paragraph, most people won't get to it.

**Google pulls featured snippets from answer-first content.** When Google shows a quick answer box at the top of search results, it's pulling from content that directly answers the query in the first sentence or two. If your content buries the answer, Google will pull from someone else's site.

**AI extracts answer-first content more reliably.** When an AI assistant builds a response, it's looking for clear, definitive statements. "Our studio is open Monday through Saturday, 6 AM to 9 PM" is infinitely easier for AI to extract than "We have flexible hours to accommodate your busy schedule, with availability throughout the week."

Format every important piece of information on your site this way:
1. Direct answer in the first sentence
2. Supporting details in the next sentence or two
3. Nuances or exceptions after that

## Why might Google not be surfacing my answers?

You might have a great FAQ page, but if Google isn't showing it in search results for the relevant queries, people will never find it through search.

Common reasons Google doesn't surface your content:

- **No schema markup.** Without FAQPage schema, Google doesn't know your page contains structured Q&A content. It's just another page of text.
- **Weak title tags and meta descriptions.** If your FAQ page title tag just says "FAQ" and the meta description is blank, Google has little context about what questions it answers.
- **Thin content.** Answers that are one sentence long don't give Google enough to work with. Each answer should be a solid, useful paragraph — not just a yes or no.
- **No internal links.** If nothing else on your site links to your FAQ page, Google may not crawl it often (or at all).
- **The content doesn't match search language.** If your customers search "do you take insurance" but your FAQ says "payment methods accepted," there's a language mismatch. Use the words your customers actually use.

## What should I fix first?

Here's a prioritized list:

1. **List your top 10 most-asked questions.** Go through your inbox, call log, and DMs. Write them down word for word — the way customers phrase them, not the way you'd phrase them.

2. **Create or improve your FAQ page.** Write a clear, answer-first response for each question. Group by topic if you have more than 10.

3. **Add FAQPage schema markup** to your FAQ page.

4. **Check your navigation.** Can someone find your hours, pricing, and service info within 10 seconds? If not, restructure.

5. **Add internal links.** Go through your main pages and link to relevant deeper pages. Make sure your FAQ page is linked from at least your homepage and your main navigation.

6. **Format for scanners.** Break up long paragraphs. Use headings, bold text, and short bullet lists to make key information pop.

## The takeaway

When customers ask questions your website already answers, the problem isn't the customer and it isn't your content. It's the gap between the two. That gap is caused by buried information, poor navigation, walls of unformatted text, and missing technical signals that prevent Google and AI from surfacing your answers. Closing this gap is one of the most practical, immediate things you can do to improve your customer experience and reduce the time you spend answering the same questions on repeat. Fix the structure, format for scanners, and help search engines find your answers — the repetitive questions will start to dry up.
