# People Stopped Opening My Emails Months Ago

You log into your email platform and look at the numbers. 12% open rate. 8% on the last one. You used to get 30% or higher. Now you're sending emails into a void, and you're starting to wonder if email even works anymore.

It does. Email still has the highest ROI of any marketing channel for small businesses. The problem isn't the channel — it's what you're sending, who you're sending it to, and how often you're showing up in people's inboxes.

Let's figure out what went wrong and how to fix it.

## Why did my open rates drop?

There's rarely a single reason. It's usually a combination of factors that compound over time. Here are the most common ones:

**Your subject lines are boring.** If every email you send has a subject line like "Monthly Newsletter — May 2026" or "Company Update," people are going to scroll right past it. Your subject line is competing with 50 to 100 other emails that day. If it doesn't earn a click, nothing else matters.

**You're sending too often — or not enough.** Both ends of the spectrum kill engagement. Send daily and people tune you out. Send once every three months and they forget who you are. For most small businesses, one to two emails per week is the sweet spot. Once a week is perfectly fine.

**Your content doesn't match why people signed up.** If someone joined your list for tips on home renovation and you're sending them company news and product promotions every week, they're going to disengage. People subscribed for a reason. If you've drifted from that reason, they'll drift away too.

**Your list is full of dead weight.** Email addresses that never open, never click, never engage. They're still on your list, dragging down your metrics and hurting your deliverability. More on that in a minute.

## What makes a subject line worth clicking?

Good subject lines do one of three things: create curiosity, promise a specific benefit, or feel personal. Great ones do two of those at once.

Some principles that work:

- **Keep it short.** 6 to 10 words is ideal. Most people are reading on their phones. Long subject lines get cut off.
- **Be specific.** "5 ways to cut your energy bill this summer" beats "Tips for saving money" every time.
- **Use their name sparingly.** Personalization tokens (like "Chase, here's your weekly update") can boost opens, but only if you don't overuse them. If every email starts with their name, it stops feeling personal and starts feeling automated.
- **Ask a question.** "Are you making this common invoicing mistake?" works because people can't help but wonder if the answer is yes.
- **Avoid spam triggers.** All caps, excessive punctuation, words like "FREE" or "ACT NOW" — these don't just look spammy, they can literally send your email to the spam folder.

Test your subject lines. Most email platforms let you A/B test. Send version A to half your list and version B to the other half. Over time, you'll learn exactly what your audience responds to.

## How often should I actually be emailing my list?

There's no universal answer, but there are guardrails.

**Minimum:** Once a month. Any less and people forget they signed up. When your next email finally arrives, they'll hit unsubscribe because they don't recognize you.

**Maximum:** Three times a week for most small businesses. Unless you're a daily newsletter (which is its own category), more than three sends per week leads to fatigue and unsubscribes.

**Sweet spot for most:** Once a week. It's frequent enough to stay top of mind, infrequent enough that each email can actually be worth reading.

Consistency matters more than frequency. If you can only commit to twice a month, do that reliably. A predictable schedule builds trust. An erratic one — three emails this week, then nothing for six weeks — trains people to ignore you.

## What is list hygiene and why should I care?

List hygiene is the practice of regularly cleaning your email list by removing subscribers who aren't engaging. It sounds counterintuitive — why would you shrink your list on purpose?

Because a smaller, engaged list outperforms a large, dead one in every way.

Here's what happens when you keep dead subscribers around:

- **Your open rates tank.** If 40% of your list never opens anything, your overall open rate is mathematically depressed. It makes your emails look worse than they are.
- **Your deliverability suffers.** Email providers like Gmail and Outlook watch engagement signals. If a large chunk of your recipients never open your emails, those providers start routing you to spam — for everyone, not just the inactive ones.
- **You're paying for nothing.** Most email platforms charge based on subscriber count. Keeping 2,000 dead addresses on your list is literally costing you money every month.

How to clean your list:

1. Identify everyone who hasn't opened or clicked in the last 90 days.
2. Send them a re-engagement campaign (two to three emails over two weeks — "Still interested? Here's what you've been missing").
3. Anyone who still doesn't engage after the re-engagement sequence gets removed.
4. Do this quarterly.

It feels painful the first time. You'll go from 5,000 subscribers to 3,200 and wonder if you made a mistake. Then you'll watch your open rates jump from 15% to 28% and you'll never look back.

## What does segmentation mean in practice?

Segmentation is splitting your list into groups based on shared characteristics so you can send more relevant emails to each group. It's the difference between blasting the same message to everyone and sending the right message to the right people.

You don't need complex segments. Start with these:

- **New subscribers vs. longtime subscribers.** New people need different content than someone who's been with you for two years.
- **Buyers vs. non-buyers.** Someone who's purchased from you should get different emails than someone who signed up for a freebie and never bought.
- **Engagement level.** Your most active openers and clickers are your VIPs. Treat them differently — early access, exclusive offers, first look at new things.
- **Interest-based.** If you offer multiple services, let people self-select what they care about. A landscaping company's customers who only want lawn care don't need emails about hardscaping every week.

Even one level of segmentation — say, buyers vs. non-buyers — can improve your click-through rates significantly. You don't have to segment everything on day one.

## When should I let go of a dead subscriber?

After you've given them a fair chance to re-engage and they haven't, it's time. The general rule: if someone hasn't opened a single email in 90 days and didn't respond to your re-engagement campaign, remove them.

Some people hold on because they think a big list number matters. It doesn't. What matters is the percentage of that list that actually reads what you send. A list of 800 people where 300 open every email is exponentially more valuable than a list of 5,000 where 200 open.

Let the dead weight go. Your deliverability, your metrics, and your sanity will all improve.

## How do I know if my emails are actually reaching inboxes?

Deliverability is whether your emails make it to the inbox or get filtered to spam or promotions. A few things that affect it:

- **Authenticate your domain.** Set up SPF, DKIM, and DMARC records for your sending domain. Your email platform will walk you through this. It tells email providers that you're a legitimate sender.
- **Use a recognizable sender name.** "Chase from TheMerchantGuide" is better than "noreply@company.com." People open emails from people, not from faceless addresses.
- **Watch your bounce rate.** If emails keep bouncing (invalid addresses), clean them immediately. High bounce rates are a red flag to email providers.
- **Don't buy email lists.** Ever. Sending to people who didn't opt in is the fastest way to destroy your sender reputation.

If you suspect deliverability issues, send a test email to your own Gmail, Outlook, and Yahoo accounts. Check where it lands. If it's going to spam, you have work to do on the technical side before worrying about content.

## The takeaway

People didn't stop caring about email. They stopped caring about emails that aren't worth their time. Fix your subject lines so they earn the click. Clean your list so you're talking to people who actually want to hear from you. Send consistently but not excessively. And segment when you can so the right message reaches the right person.

Start with a list cleanup this week. Remove the dead weight, send a re-engagement campaign to the borderline subscribers, and watch what happens to your open rates. You'll be surprised how much better your email performs when you stop trying to reach everyone and focus on reaching the right ones.
